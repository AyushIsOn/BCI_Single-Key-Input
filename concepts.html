Circular Keyboards for a Neuralink-Driven Cursor

A thought-experiment gallery. No optimization, no benchmarks — just reasoning from what a brain-controlled cursor actually is up to the shape on the screen. Each sketch animates the core idea so you can feel how it would move.

▸ Hover any card. The animations loop. Everything here is conceptual.

The constraint that drives every design

  • The cursor is a noisy 2D point driven by imagined movement — continuous, not discrete.
  • Holding a steady direction is the costly, tiring act. Being in motion is comparatively cheap.
  • "Clicking" is a separate, expensive gesture — so "point, then click" pays twice.
  • There is no haptic feedback and no physical home; the screen is the only ground truth.
  • Drift and tremor punish small, tightly-packed targets.

Family 1 · Angle-first — the cursor points, geometry does the rest

Pie / Sunburst

Lean, don't travel

Slice the screen into wedges. You don't reach a key — you lean toward a direction and whichever wedge owns your heading lights up. Selection is "heading held for a moment."

Why it fits: angle is easier to control than distance, and sloppy aim still lands in the right fat wedge.

The Marble-Roll

Rest is free

The keyboard is a shallow bowl, the cursor a marble. Tilt your intention and it rolls toward a rim letter. Let go and it falls back to the center valley — returning to neutral costs nothing.

Why it fits: the biggest fatigue win is making "rest" automatic instead of another deliberate move.

Radar Sweep

2D collapsed to 1D

A single rotating hand, like a clock. You only control its speed (up/down intent) and "drop" it when it points at your letter. The hardest dimension — precise landing — is removed entirely.

Trade-off: maximally robust to noise, but slow. Good for the worst-signal days.

Family 2 · Continuous flow — the cursor never stops

Dasher-in-a-Circle

Fly through a probability field

Letters spiral inward. You steer toward a region of the alphabet; as you fly in it magnifies and the next letters bloom. Prediction makes likely letters huge, unlikely ones tiny. You never "select" — you glide.

Why it fits: no stopping, no clicking. Writing becomes one long curving motion.

The Whirlpool

The keys move, not you

Letters orbit slowly. You nudge your target letter toward the center drain and it commits when it reaches the middle. The keyboard's own motion does half the work — your cursor barely travels.

Why it fits: almost nobody moves the keyboard instead of the cursor. Huge potential fatigue lever.

Gesture-Through

Swipe a word in one loop

Draw one continuous loop that passes over a word's letters in order; prediction snaps it to the intended word. Common letters sit near the center so the loop stays small and tight.

Trade-off: fast and fluid for known words, weak for code identifiers and names.

Family 3 · Lean into the noise instead of fighting it

Radial T9 — Big Zones

Precision traded for prediction

Forget 30 tiny keys. Place 6–8 huge zones, each holding a cluster of letters. A word is a sequence of gross zone-flicks; a language model figures out which letters you meant.

Trade-off: very noise-tolerant and fast, at the cost of occasional ambiguity.

Concentric Speed Rings

Forgive imprecision where it matters

Frequent letters fill a fat inner ring you can hit carelessly; rare letters are banished to a thin outer ring you only visit deliberately. Geometry forgives sloppiness exactly where you spend most time.

Why it fits: matches target size to how often (and how carefully) you reach for it.

Magnetic Keys

The keys fight your tremor

Each key has a gravity well. As the noisy cursor wanders near, it gets gently pulled to the nearest key center, cancelling jitter. The circle looks normal — the behavior does the stabilizing.

Trade-off: wonderful when well-tuned; over-strong wells make nearby keys hard to separate.

Family 4 · Rethink what "a key" even is

Syllable / Chord Petals

Two characters per gesture

A flower where each petal is a common pair or syllable — th, ing, re, qu. One motion writes two letters. Fewer, bigger targets; faster effective speed.

Trade-off: a learning curve — but skilled users get a real speed multiplier.

Two-Layer Dial (Safe-Cracker)

Two coarse moves beat one precise one

An outer ring picks a group of letters; a second push picks within the group — like dialing a combination lock. Trades one fragile precise motion for two robust coarse ones.

Trade-off: two strokes per character, but each is forgiving.

Predictive Crown

Most typing becomes a tiny nudge

A neutral ring of letters, but the top predicted words float as giant buttons right where the cursor rests. You barely move for common words; you only venture to the ring when prediction fails — which is exactly the case for code.

Why it fits: keeps the most-used target under the resting cursor at all times.

The deeper design tensions

1 · Precision vs. prediction

The less you trust the cursor, the more you must lean on a language model. Code breaks high-prediction designs — its symbols and identifiers lack natural-language redundancy.

2 · Discrete vs. continuous

Land-and-click is intuitive but costs a separate gesture per character. Flow designs feel alien at first but can be far less tiring over a long session.

3 · Where does "rest" live?

The most underrated question. If returning to neutral is free (a bowl bottom, a gravity center), the whole interface feels effortless. If neutral is just another spot to steer to, fatigue compounds.

4 · Does the keyboard move, or only the cursor?

Whirlpool-style designs move the keys toward a fixed commit point so the brain does less work. Almost nobody explores this — it may be the biggest lever for a fatigue-limited device.

If I had to bet on the most Neuralink-native feel

Not the fastest — the most humane: a bowl-shaped, gravity-centered, continuous-flow dial. The cursor rests in the middle for free. You lean toward a region; letters bloom and magnify as you flow toward them; prediction inflates the likely ones; you never stop or click. Writing feels like steering a glider, not stabbing at keys.
